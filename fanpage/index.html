Haring's Rise to Fame

Check out some photos of Haring and his work!

Haring got his start as an artist in the New York City subways in the 1980s after moving to the Lower East Side to study painting at the School of Visual Arts in 1978. His early work came from graffiti culture where he would create white chalk drawings on black unused subway advertisement boards. It was in his subway creations where certain images and iconography became his trademark, making his work more recognizable. To this day, his symbols like the barking dog or the Radiant Baby, which became his tage to sign his subway drawings, have left a language in the legacy of his artwork. Haring then moved from commentary on the streets and in the subways to painting larger murals, paintings, and he even worked with fashion designers or popstars. Quickly becoming part of the art scene, Haring rose to high fame with his worked featured in numerous exhibits and lots of attention from the media.

Visit the Keith Haring Wikipedia page to learn more.

Haring's Late Work and Death

After a long, successful career with more than 100 solo exhibitions and more than 50 public works, Haring became more focused on activism and social messages toward the end of his career. As an openly gay man, he advocated primarily for safe sex and AIDS awareness, but included other messages as well, like his well known "Crack is Wack" mural. Haring died in 1990 at the age of 31 due to AIDS related complications. Since then he has been honored around the world by numerous LGBTQ organizations, musicians, companies that adpated his work into products, and he has been featured in a number of documentaries. Haring became an icon in the American art scene of street and pop art, leaving a legacy as an activist and brilliant creative.
